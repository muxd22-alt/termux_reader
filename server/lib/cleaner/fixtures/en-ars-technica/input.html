The Race to Build Quantum-Resistant Encryption

Dan GoodinApril 5, 2024

As quantum computers grow more powerful, the cryptographic algorithms that protect everything from banking transactions to military communications face an existential threat. The race to develop quantum-resistant encryption has never been more urgent.

In August 2024, NIST finalized its first set of post-quantum cryptography standards, marking a watershed moment in the field. The three selected algorithms — ML-KEM (formerly CRYSTALS-Kyber), ML-DSA (formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium), and SLH-DSA (formerly SPHINCS+) — represent years of rigorous analysis and competition.

The Quantum Threat

Shor's algorithm, when run on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer, can factor large numbers exponentially faster than any known classical algorithm. This directly threatens RSA and elliptic curve cryptography, which underpin virtually all secure communications on the internet.

While current quantum computers are far from being able to break modern encryption, the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat is real. Adversaries can intercept and store encrypted data today, waiting for quantum computers powerful enough to decrypt it in the future.

Lattice-Based Cryptography

The majority of NIST's selected algorithms are based on lattice problems — mathematical structures that are believed to be resistant to both classical and quantum attacks. The Shortest Vector Problem (SVP) and its variants form the security foundation of these schemes.

Migration Challenges

Migrating existing systems to post-quantum cryptography is a massive undertaking. Organizations must inventory their cryptographic assets, prioritize systems handling long-lived secrets, and test compatibility with the new algorithms.
